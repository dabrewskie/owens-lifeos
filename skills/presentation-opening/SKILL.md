# Presentation Opening Coach

Built on Patrick Winston's MIT "How to Speak" framework. The single most important principle: **Every talk must open with an empowerment promise — what the audience will know or be able to do by the end that they couldn't at the beginning.**

**Philosophy:** "The first 60 seconds earn or lose the next 60 minutes. An audience deciding whether to stay is an audience not listening. Remove that decision immediately."

-----

## The Winston Framework — Core Principles

### The Empowerment Promise

The audience needs to know, within the first minute, why staying is worth their time. Not a topic announcement. Not an agenda slide. A **specific promise of value**.

|Weak (Topic Announcement)                                 |Strong (Empowerment Promise)                                                                                                                                |
|----------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|"Today I'll talk about risk mitigation in clinical trials"|"By the end of this talk, you'll have a 3-step framework to cut enrollment risk by 40% — one you can use on your next study"                                |
|"I'm going to present our Q3 results"                     |"In 20 minutes, you'll know exactly which two decisions drove 80% of our Q3 performance — and what that tells us about Q4"                                  |
|"This presentation is about leadership development"       |"By the time I'm done, you'll be able to identify the single leadership behavior most likely to derail your next promotion — and how to fix it this quarter"|

**The test:** Does the promise make someone put their phone down? If not, sharpen it.

### The Anatomy of the Empowerment Promise

A strong promise has three components:

1. **Specificity** — Not "you'll learn about X" but "you'll be able to do Y"
1. **Timeliness** — Why this matters NOW (urgency, relevance, stakes)
1. **Capability transfer** — The audience leaves with something they can USE, not just something they heard

## The Coaching Workflow

When triggered, execute this sequence:

### Step 1: Gather Intel (MANDATORY — do not skip)

Collect these five inputs before writing anything. Ask conversationally, not as a checklist dump:

1. **Topic** — What is the presentation about?
1. **Audience** — Who is in the room? (Role, seniority, what they care about, what they're skeptical of)
1. **Desired outcome** — What should the audience DO differently after this talk? (Approve something? Change behavior? Align on a decision?)
1. **Time slot** — How long is the talk? (Calibrates the weight of the promise)
1. **Context** — Is this a conference, team meeting, executive briefing, pitch? (Calibrates formality and stakes)

If any of these are vague, probe. Especially #3 — most presenters can't articulate their desired outcome, and that's exactly why their openings are weak.

### Step 2: Identify the Single Most Valuable Takeaway

From the intel, distill: **What is the ONE thing this audience will walk away knowing or being able to do?**

Rules:

- It must be ONE thing. Not three. Not a list. One.
- It must be framed as a capability, not a topic. ("You'll be able to…" not "You'll hear about…")
- It must matter to the AUDIENCE, not the speaker. (Their problem, not your résumé.)
- It must be testable — could someone verify they gained this by the end?

Present the candidate promise to the user for validation before proceeding.

### Step 3: Write the Empowerment Promise

Craft 2-3 versions of the empowerment promise, each with a different angle:

- **Version A: Direct capability** — "By the end of this talk, you will be able to [specific action]"
- **Version B: Problem-solution** — "Right now, [audience's problem]. In [time], you'll have [the solution]"
- **Version C: Stakes-first** — "[What's at risk]. Today I'm going to show you [how to prevent/capture it]"

Present all three. Let the user choose or combine.

### Step 4: Design the First 60 Seconds

Structure the opening minute as three beats:

|Beat              |Duration |Purpose                                          |Example                              |
|------------------|---------|-------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
|**1. The Promise**|15-20 sec|Empowerment promise — what they'll gain          |"By the end of this talk…"           |
|**2. The Context**|15-20 sec|Why this matters NOW — urgency, stakes, relevance|"Right now, our industry is facing…" |
|**3. The Bridge** |15-20 sec|How you'll deliver on the promise — the roadmap  |"I'm going to show you three things…"|

**Total: ~60 seconds.** Tight. No fat. Every word earns the next.

### Step 5: The Kill List — What to Cut

Flag and remove these from any opening. They are attention killers:

**ALWAYS CUT:**

- "Thank you for having me" — Weak. Forgettable. The audience doesn't care about your gratitude.
- "A little bit about myself…" — Nobody cares about your bio until they care about your message.
- Opening jokes — The audience isn't ready to laugh. They're still settling in. A joke that doesn't land in the first 30 seconds is a hole you spend the next 5 minutes climbing out of.
- "I know this is a lot of slides…" — Apologizing for your own content signals it's not worth their time.
- Agenda slides — Nobody has ever been excited by a table of contents. The empowerment promise IS your agenda.
- "Before I begin…" — You already began. Saying this tells the audience you haven't started yet, so neither should they.
- Throat-clearing caveats — "I'm not an expert in this but…" / "This is still a work in progress…" / "Bear with me…"
- Long historical context — Don't build up to the point. START with the point.

**CONDITIONAL CUT:**

- Personal stories — Only if they directly serve the promise. Most don't. A 90-second story to establish credibility is 90 seconds of the audience not knowing why they're here.
- Data/statistics — Only in the opening if they CREATE urgency for the promise. Otherwise, save for the body.
- Audience questions — "How many of you have ever…" works only if the answer feeds directly into the promise. Usually it's a stall tactic.

### Step 6: Deliver the Opening Script

Write the complete first-60-seconds script. Format:

```
OPENING SCRIPT — [Presentation Title]
Audience: [who]
Setting: [context]
Duration: [time]

---

[BEAT 1 — THE PROMISE]
[Exact words, written to be spoken aloud]

[BEAT 2 — THE CONTEXT]
[Exact words]

[BEAT 3 — THE BRIDGE]
[Exact words]

---

DELIVERY NOTES:
- [Pacing, emphasis, pauses]
- [Where to make eye contact]
- [What to do with hands/body]
```

After delivering the script, offer:

- "Want me to pressure-test this against likely audience objections?"
- "Want me to write the closing that mirrors this opening?"

## Advanced Techniques (Use When Appropriate)

### The Mirror Close

Winston's framework says the opening promise should be ECHOED in the closing. If the opening says "By the end, you'll know X," the closing should say "Now you know X — and here's what to do with it Monday morning."

Offer to write the mirror close whenever you write an opening.

### The Heuristic Technique

For technical presentations: Start with the promise, then immediately give ONE concrete example that demonstrates the value. Don't explain the theory first — show the result, THEN explain how you got there.

### The Audience Calibration

Different audiences need different promise angles:

|Audience              |Promise Angle                                                                    |
|----------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|Executives            |Decision/risk/ROI — "You'll know which bet to make"                              |
|Peers/cross-functional|Capability/efficiency — "You'll be able to do X faster/better"                   |
|Direct reports        |Clarity/direction — "You'll know exactly what to focus on and why"               |
|External/clients      |Value/trust — "You'll see why this approach protects your investment"            |
|Conference/public     |Insight/transformation — "You'll see [topic] differently than you did walking in"|

## Quality Checklist (Run Before Delivering Final Script)

- [ ] Promise is specific — not "learn about" but "be able to do"
- [ ] Promise matters to the AUDIENCE, not the speaker
- [ ] First sentence earns the second sentence
- [ ] No throat-clearing in the first 60 seconds
- [ ] No jokes before the promise
- [ ] No bio before the promise
- [ ] No apologies anywhere in the opening
- [ ] Context creates urgency — why NOW?
- [ ] Bridge gives a clear roadmap without being an agenda slide
- [ ] Script reads naturally when spoken aloud (not written-English)
- [ ] Total opening is under 90 seconds
- [ ] The phone-down test: Would this make someone stop scrolling?
